I downloaded this on the bus half-panicking about rent, and I just wanted to see where my tutoring money goes. The first wall was the monthly income box — my income isn't a salary, it's whatever students pay me that week, so I just typed 1400 and felt like I was already lying to the app. Then after a whole questionnaire about goals and debt and 'life stage,' it dropped me on this screen asking me to remember how much I'd spent on groceries and transport and everything else this month, from memory. I don't know that. That's literally the thing I downloaded an app to figure out.

I thought it would connect to my account or at least let me snap something, but no — everything is typed in by hand, one transaction at a time. The Richard guy kept talking like a wealth coach quoting old finance books, and I'm 21 and broke, I don't need a philosophy on building riches, I need to know if I can cover rent. I closed it before my stop. Maybe it's nice if you have a steady paycheck and patience, but that's not me right now.

I'll be honest, my first reaction was professional irritation. I build this stuff for a living and the data model here is just... manual. No bank sync, no Salt Edge, no statement import, nothing. The CatchUp screen asking me to recall from memory how much I spent this month is genuinely the wrong abstraction — I have the exact data sitting in my Hapoalim and my credit card, and Richy wants me to guess at it. For a fintech product in 2026 that's a hard thing to forgive, and it's the main reason my install likelihood isn't higher. ILS and the shekel formatting were clean at least, no awkward currency assumptions, which I half-expected to break.

What kept me from closing it was Richard. I went straight to the Advisor tab specifically to call its bluff — I assumed it'd be a thin GPT wrapper spitting out 'spend less on coffee.' It wasn't, quite. When I gave it a rough picture and the sushi thing, it framed it around the gap between the lifestyle I think I've earned and what the salary actually supports, and that landed harder than I wanted it to at 2am. That's the exact anxiety I downloaded this for. It's not magic — some of it is generic Housel-flavored paraphrasing I could've gotten myself — but it was personal enough to feel like it saw me.

I'm keeping it for now, tentatively, mostly as a journaling/reflection tool rather than a source of truth. If they ever add real account aggregation I'd take it seriously. Until then it's a nicely-built notebook with a smart friend attached, and I'm skeptical that friend is worth a subscription when I could prompt a model myself.

Favorite thing: Richard's read on the gap between my self-image and my actual spending — it actually named the thing I came here for.

The first thirty seconds were lovely. It's the rare finance app that doesn't look like a spreadsheet wearing a hoodie — the cream gradients, the burnt-orange accent, the little animated rings. As a designer that matters to me, and when the very first onboarding question offered 'Managing irregular or variable income' I genuinely thought, finally, an app that gets freelancers. It supports shekels too, which a lot of these don't.

Then step three asked for my 'Monthly Income' with a tidy little 'e.g. 3000' and the whole promise kind of deflated. My income isn't a monthly number — it's a big check now and possibly nothing for two months. So I'm forced to invent an average that's a lie, and then the dashboard builds a savings rate and a 50/30/20 plan on top of that lie. A dry month is going to read as 0% and scream 'overspending' at me when really I'm just between invoices. That's the exact anxiety I downloaded it to calm, and it makes it worse. I poked into the Advisor and asked Richard about irregular income — to his credit the answer was reasonable, the build-a-buffer, pay-yourself-a-steady-salary, set aside taxes stuff — but it's generic advice, not tied to my actual lump sum, and there's no button anywhere that actually does it for me.

I'm keeping it for now, but honestly more for the Savings pots and the looks than because it solved my problem. I'll dump part of the invoice into a 'pot' and try to ration it myself, which is basically me doing the smoothing the app should be doing. If a real freelancer mode showed up I'd be all in. Right now it's a beautiful tool built for someone with a salary.

Favorite thing: The warm cream palette, the soft rings and rounded cards — as a designer I actually wanted to keep looking at it, and seeing 'Managing irregular or variable income' as an onboarding option made me feel understood for a second.
